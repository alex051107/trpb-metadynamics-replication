GenSLMs: Genome-scale language models reveal SARS-CoV-2 evolutionary dynamics

Zvyagin MT, Brace A, Hippe K, Deng Y, Zhang B, Boureima CT, Clyde A, Ramanathan A, et al.
Int. J. High Perf. Comput. Appl. 2023, Vol. 37(6), 683–705 · DOI: 10.1177/10943420231201154 · bioRxiv: 10.1101/2022.10.10.511571
Lab: Ramanathan Lab (Argonne National Laboratory) + Anandkumar Lab (Caltech). PubMed ID: 36451881.
Genome Language Model Codon tokenization GPT-NeoX 25B parameters SARS-CoV-2 VOC identification Latent space Foundation model Argonne ALCF Polaris
ABS Abstract — Paper 1
ABS·1

The global pandemic caused by SARS-CoV-2 highlighted the urgent need for rapid, predictive genomic surveillance. We present GenSLMs, a family of genome-scale language models trained on over 110 million prokaryotic gene sequences that can learn the evolutionary language of SARS-CoV-2 variants. The models use codon-level tokenization — treating each three-nucleotide codon as a single token — and a GPT-style autoregressive transformer architecture (GPT-NeoX family), scaled from 25M to 25B parameters. After fine-tuning on 1.5 million SARS-CoV-2 whole-genome sequences, the resulting latent space embeddings cluster according to biologically meaningful lineages, enabling prospective identification of variants of concern (VOCs) before formal classification. Training utilized 1.63 Zettaflops of compute, achieving sustained 121 PFLOPS (peak 850 PFLOPS) on Argonne ALCF Polaris and Cerebras accelerators. GenSLMs represent one of the first genome-scale foundation models and demonstrate that language modeling at the DNA level captures evolutionary fitness information invisible to traditional phylogenetic methods.

§1 Introduction — Paper 1
P1-1

The Surveillance Problem. Traditional genomic surveillance for pandemic pathogens relies on phylogenetic methods that classify variants relative to known reference trees. These approaches are fundamentally reactive: they require a sufficient accumulation of sequences and human expert-defined lineage criteria (e.g., Pango nomenclature) before a new variant can be formally classified as a VOC. The typical lag between a variant's emergence and its formal VOC designation can span weeks to months — a delay that is epidemiologically catastrophic if that variant is more transmissible or immune-evasive. Moreover, phylogenetic methods treat sequences as identity strings: they measure genetic distance but do not capture the fitness landscape of the sequence space. They cannot predict whether a newly observed mutation combination is functionally neutral or has pandemic potential. A fundamentally different approach is needed — one that learns the underlying evolutionary logic, not just the empirical tree topology.

P1-2

Reframing as Representation Learning. GenSLM reframes pandemic surveillance as a representation-learning problem: if a language model is pretrained on the evolutionary logic of biological sequences at the DNA/codon level, its learned latent space should reflect functional relationships, evolutionary pressures, and fitness constraints — not just sequence similarity. The goal is a foundation model whose internal representations, without any explicit supervision, separate variants with pandemic potential from background neutral variation. This is analogous to how large language models for natural text learn to represent semantic meaning rather than character frequency. The key hypothesis is that the patterns of codon usage, synonymous variation, and long-range genomic correlations that constitute the "evolutionary grammar" of a pathogen genome are learnable by a transformer model, and that this learned grammar is predictive of fitness.

段落功能：提出核心重框架——"监测" = "表示学习"。这是GenSLM的哲学立场：latent space = fitness landscape的低维投影。

核心假说
与自然语言PLM的类比：就像BERT/GPT学到语义（"国王"-"男人"+"女人"≈"女王"），GenSLM应该学到生物学语义（"Alpha-type mutation" + "immune-evading pattern" ≈ "VOC-like"）。类比有局限性，但直觉上有助于理解。
与你的项目（关键！）：同样的逻辑迁移到TrpB：GenSLM fine-tuned on 30K trpB DNA sequences should have learned what makes a "valid, functional TrpB" — the evolutionary grammar of the TrpB family. Generated sequences should inherit this grammar, producing functional enzymes without DE.
§2 Methods — Paper 1
P1-3

Codon-Level Tokenization: The Key Design Choice. GenSLM tokenizes biological sequences at the codon level — each three-nucleotide codon (e.g., ATG, GCC, TAA) is treated as a single token. The biological motivation is threefold: First, the codon is the fundamental unit of the genetic code; codon-level tokenization preserves the reading frame and the mapping between DNA and protein. Second, codon usage bias — the non-uniform use of synonymous codons across organisms — encodes information about translation efficiency, tRNA availability, and organism-level expression optimization that is completely invisible to amino-acid-level models. Third, consecutive codons within a gene are not independent: they participate in mRNA secondary structure, co-translational folding constraints, and local GC content regulation. The vocabulary consists of 64 codons (61 sense + 3 stop), yielding a compact but complete tokenizer with no out-of-vocabulary tokens for any valid coding sequence. Nucleotide-level tokenization (4 tokens) would not capture reading frame information; amino-acid-level tokenization (20 tokens) would lose all synonymous variation.

P1-4

Pretraining Scale and Architecture. The pretraining dataset consists of over 110 million prokaryotic gene sequences from BV-BRC (Bacterial and Viral Bioinformatics Resource Center), covering an enormous diversity of organisms, environments, and evolutionary lineages. Three model scales were trained: 25M, 2.5B, and 25B parameters, following the GPT-NeoX architecture with rotary positional embeddings, which are more suitable for long sequences than absolute positional encodings. The maximum context length for prokaryotic pretraining is 2,048 tokens; for SARS-CoV-2 fine-tuning, this was extended to 10,240 tokens to accommodate whole-genome sequences (~30 kb = ~10,000 codons). For whole-genome modeling, a hierarchical architecture was adopted: a diffusion model at the genome level handles long-range context, while the bottom-level GPT-NeoX transformer operates on individual gene subsequences. Training achieved 1.63 Zettaflops total compute, sustained 121 PFLOPS (mixed precision), peak 850 PFLOPS across Argonne ALCF Polaris supercomputer and Cerebras wafer-scale AI accelerators.

P1-5

Fine-tuning and Downstream Evaluation. Fine-tuning was performed on 1.5 million SARS-CoV-2 whole-genome sequences, using the same causal language modeling objective (next-codon prediction) without any explicit variant labels. This is the crucial property of the approach: the model is never told "this sequence is Delta" or "this sequence is Omicron." It learns the statistical structure of the sequence space entirely unsupervised. For downstream evaluation, embeddings (latent representations) are extracted from intermediate transformer layers, then projected to 2D using UMAP for visualization. The key test design is prospective: the model is trained on sequences from December 2019 to December 2020 (Alpha, Beta variants), then evaluated on sequences from January 2021 to April 2022 (Delta, Omicron, Iota) — variants the model has never seen during training or fine-tuning.

P1-5

段落功能：描述评估协议——关键是"前瞻性"设计：用2020年之前的数据训练，测试模型能否识别2021年之后的新变体。这是比回顾性分析强得多的验证方式。

§3 Results — Paper 1
P1-6

Latent Space Structure. UMAP projections of GenSLM embeddings from the fine-tuned model show clear, biologically meaningful clustering of SARS-CoV-2 lineages. Alpha sequences cluster together; Beta sequences form a distinct cluster; Omicron sequences — which were not seen during fine-tuning — form their own cluster, separated from the others by large latent-space distance. Crucially, the UMAP projection recapitulates the known phylogenetic relationships of these lineages, including intermediate positions for recombinant variants, without using any explicit phylogenetic information. The z-score of pairwise distances between VOC clusters versus background variants is significantly elevated, confirming that VOC sequences occupy distinct, non-overlapping regions of latent space. This clustering emerges purely from the model's learned representation of codon patterns, synonymous variation structure, and long-range genomic correlations.

P1-7

Prospective VOC Identification. GenSLM correctly separated Delta, Omicron, and Iota from background variants using embeddings from a model trained exclusively on pre-2021 data, without explicit VOC labels. When embeddings of newly-sequenced (January 2021 onward) sequences are projected into the latent space, VOC-destined sequences occupy distinct, well-separated clusters far from the bulk of background variants, weeks to months before formal WHO VOC designation. Iota variants, which were ultimately not designated a VOC but showed local transmissibility advantages, occupy an intermediate position — correctly reflecting their intermediate fitness status. The silhouette score of the VOC clusters exceeds 0.7, indicating well-separated latent space structure. This prospective identification property is the primary demonstration of GenSLM's utility: it represents a shift from reactive (classify after enough sequences) to proactive (flag potential VOC sequences as they emerge) surveillance.

§4 Discussion — Paper 1
P1-8

Transfer Potential to Protein Engineering. The key architectural property that makes GenSLM transferable to protein engineering is its operation at the DNA/codon level, not the amino-acid level. While protein-centric language models (ESM-2, ProtTrans, ProteinBERT) operate on amino acid sequences, GenSLM operates on the underlying genetic code. This distinction matters for three reasons: First, codon bias encodes host expression efficiency — in heterologous expression systems (e.g., E. coli), codon usage patterns directly impact translation rate and protein yield. Second, synonymous codon variation captures population-level sequence diversity not visible in protein sequences — natural diversity at the DNA level exceeds that at the protein level. Third, the model learns correlations across the full reading frame, not just within the protein fold, potentially capturing regulatory signals that affect expression levels in vivo. The pretraining on 110M prokaryotic genes means the model has implicitly learned what a "valid gene" looks like in terms of codon usage, open reading frame structure, and sequence constraints — making fine-tuning on a specific gene family (e.g., trpB) a highly efficient specialization step.

ABS Abstract — Paper 2
ABS·2

The directed evolution of enzymes for non-natural reactions requires a starting point with measurable activity, limiting its applicability to truly novel chemistries. Here we show that a generative language model trained on DNA sequences — GenSLM — can produce novel tryptophan synthase β-subunit (TrpB) enzymes with functional properties out of the box, without any directed evolution rounds. We fine-tuned the 25M parameter GenSLM on ~30,000 unique trpB nucleotide sequences, then generated approximately 60,000 candidate sequences. After multi-stage filtering (start-token, length, sequence identity, ESMFold pLDDT ≥ 80), a final set was synthesized (Twist Bioscience) and expressed in E. coli. The most active variant, GenSLM-230, exceeds the activity of PfTrpB-0B2 — a variant produced by multiple rounds of directed evolution — in both native L-tryptophan synthesis and non-canonical substrate reactions. More than 7 generated enzymes retained substantial activity at 75°C, despite thermostability not being an explicit design target. These results demonstrate that generative AI trained on DNA sequences can explore unexplored sequence space and produce enzymes with properties absent from the closest natural homologs, providing a powerful complement to directed evolution for enzyme engineering.

§5 Introduction — Paper 2
P2-1

The Directed Evolution Bottleneck. Directed evolution (DE) is the gold standard for enzyme engineering, having produced catalysts for pharmaceuticals, specialty chemicals, and materials with remarkable efficiency. However, DE requires a starting enzyme with measurable activity on the desired substrate or reaction type. For entirely novel chemistries — reactions with no known natural enzyme — this starting point does not exist by definition, creating a fundamental barrier. Furthermore, even when a starting point exists, DE proceeds through incremental mutations in a greedy hill-climbing fashion: it explores only the local sequence neighborhood of the starting enzyme, and each round requires expensive library construction, transformation, expression, and high-throughput screening. The process is inherently reactive and labor-intensive. An alternative that can generate diverse functional starting points from scratch — bypassing the need for an initial hit — would fundamentally transform the enzyme engineering paradigm.

段落功能：建立DE的两个根本局限：①需要有活性起点（无法从零开始）；②局部探索（每轮只走一小步，错过序列空间的全局最优）。这是GenSLM方法的motivation。

P2-2

The AI Design Proposition. We propose that a generative language model trained on a large corpus of natural DNA sequences from a target enzyme family has implicitly learned the evolutionary constraints that make sequences in that family functional: the conserved residues, the tolerated variation patterns, the codon usage biases, and the long-range sequence correlations that constitute a "valid enzyme." Sampling from this model's learned distribution therefore produces new sequences that satisfy these implicit constraints — sequences that should be functional without requiring any experimental starting point or optimization. This proposition makes a testable prediction: generated sequences should (1) express in a heterologous host, (2) fold into the target enzyme architecture, and (3) display catalytic activity on the native substrate. The degree to which generated sequences exceed the natural distribution defines the extent of "creative exploration" beyond what evolution has already sampled.

§6 Methods — Paper 2
P2-3

GenSLM Fine-tuning on TrpB. The base model is the 25M parameter GenSLM checkpoint pretrained on 110M prokaryotic genes. The choice of the 25M variant (rather than 2.5B or 25B) was deliberate: for a task involving a single enzyme family (~30K sequences, ~1,100 bp each), the smaller model offers faster iteration cycles, lower risk of overfitting to the limited fine-tuning data, and more tractable compute requirements. The fine-tuning dataset consists of ~30,000 unique trpB nucleotide sequences (corresponding to ~22,800 unique amino acid sequences), sourced from natural sequence databases. The nucleotide/amino acid discrepancy reflects the information available exclusively at the DNA level — synonymous codons that appear different at the DNA level but map to the same protein sequence. Fine-tuning used the same causal language modeling objective: given codons 1...n, predict codon n+1. No catalytic activity data, no structural data, and no experimental labels were used — the entire fine-tuning is sequence-only.

P2-4

Generation and Multi-stage Filtering Pipeline. Approximately 60,000 TrpB candidate sequences were generated by autoregressive sampling from the fine-tuned model, starting from an ATG codon (start codon prompt). These candidates were then subjected to a hierarchical filtering pipeline, applying cheap computational filters first:

  1. Start-token filter: Retain only sequences beginning with ATG (Met). Autoregressive models are sensitive to the first token; non-ATG starts indicate generation failure modes.
  2. Length filter: Retain sequences within 2 standard deviations of the natural TrpB mean length (363.6 ± 57.9 amino acids). Excludes truncated or extended sequences.
  3. Sequence identity filter: Balance novelty and functionality by excluding trivial copies of training data; retain sequences that explore genuine sequence space novelty.
  4. ESMFold pLDDT filter: Run ESMFold structure prediction; retain only sequences with mean pLDDT ≥ 80, indicating confident structural prediction and expected foldability.

After all filters, a small final candidate set was selected for gene synthesis and experimental validation.

§7 Results — Paper 2
P2-6

GenSLM-230 Exceeds the Directed Evolution Benchmark. GenSLM-230, the most active of the generated TrpB variants, displays catalytic activity exceeding that of PfTrpB-0B2 in the native L-serine + indole → L-tryptophan reaction, without any directed evolution rounds. This is a remarkable result: PfTrpB-0B2 required multiple rounds of directed evolution and high-throughput screening by the Arnold group to achieve its stand-alone activity; GenSLM-230 was generated from scratch by a language model and expressed in E. coli. GenSLM-230 displays stand-alone TrpB function — high activity in the absence of TrpA — recapitulating the property that required DE to achieve in natural TrpBs. Sequence analysis reveals that GenSLM-230 shares approximately 80.5% amino acid sequence identity with NdTrpB (Neobacillus drentensis TrpB), its closest natural homolog — yet it clearly surpasses NdTrpB in activity, confirming that the performance improvement is not simply inherited from the closest natural sequence.

P2-7

Substrate Promiscuity Panel. Multiple GenSLM-generated TrpBs displayed enhanced substrate promiscuity compared to wild-type TrpBs, accepting non-canonical nucleophiles and electrophiles that natural TrpBs reject or react with poorly. The promiscuity panel included: non-canonical nucleophiles (D-serine and other amino acid analogs replacing L-serine); non-canonical electrophiles (halogenated indoles, methyl-substituted indoles). Several generated variants showed substantially higher relative yields with non-canonical substrates compared to PfTrpB WT and even PfTrpB-0B2. Critically, this enhanced promiscuity was not an explicit design target — it emerged spontaneously from the generation process. This suggests that the generated sequences have not merely optimized for the native reaction but have genuinely explored new functional territory in enzyme-substrate space. The structural basis for this promiscuity is not explained in the paper — no crystal structures or detailed active-site analysis of the generated variants were provided — leaving the molecular mechanism entirely open.

P2-8

Thermostability: An Emergent Property. At least 7 generated TrpB variants retained substantial catalytic activity at 75°C, a temperature relevant to the natural hyperthermophile PfTrpB (from Pyrococcus furiosus, optimal growth 100°C). This is striking because thermostability was never specified as a design objective during GenSLM fine-tuning. The fine-tuning dataset (~30,000 trpB sequences) likely spans organisms across a wide temperature range — mesophiles, thermophiles, and hyperthermophiles. The model appears to have learned the sequence correlates of thermostability as part of the general TrpB sequence grammar, and some generated sequences spontaneously inherit thermostable-associated codon and amino acid patterns. This emergent thermostability is an important property for industrial applications: high-temperature reactions accelerate reaction rates, reduce contamination risk, and enable coupling with thermophilic cofactor regeneration systems.

P2-9

NdTrpB: The Closest Natural Homolog Comparison. GenSLM-230 shares approximately 80.5% amino acid sequence identity with NdTrpB (TrpB from Neobacillus drentensis), making NdTrpB the closest known natural homolog to the most active generated variant. This comparison is designed to control for the possibility that GenSLM-230's activity simply inherits from its natural neighbor rather than representing genuine AI-driven novelty. The result is clear: NdTrpB, despite sharing 80.5% identity with GenSLM-230, displays substantially lower activity in the promiscuity panel and on native substrates. The ~19.5% sequence difference between GenSLM-230 and NdTrpB corresponds to roughly 70 amino acid substitutions across the ~360 residue protein. The locations and functional consequences of these substitutions are not mapped in the paper, leaving open the question of which specific sequence differences drive the enhanced activity. Identifying these functionally critical differences between GenSLM-230 and NdTrpB — through structural analysis and/or MetaDynamics — is a direct future research question.

§8 Discussion — Paper 2
P2-10

Exploring Uncharted Sequence Space. The most significant conceptual contribution of this work is demonstrating that a generative language model trained on natural sequences can produce enzymes with properties not present in the closest natural homologs — the model is genuinely creative, not merely interpolating. The sequence identity range of active generated variants (most at 80–90% identity to closest natural TrpB, some at 70–80%) places them in a regime that is accessible to DE only after many rounds of optimization. Generating functional enzymes at 70–80% identity to the nearest natural sequence in a single pass is qualitatively different from DE, which typically accumulates 5–20 mutations per round. The model has effectively compressed what would require dozens of DE rounds into a single sampling operation. The DNA-level training is proposed as the key enabler: by operating at the codon level, GenSLM can simultaneously optimize amino acid identity, codon usage for E. coli expression, and long-range sequence correlations — constraints that cannot all be simultaneously optimized by amino-acid-level PLMs.

P2-11

Limitations and Open Questions. The paper acknowledges several important limitations and leaves key questions open. The multi-stage filtering pipeline is described qualitatively — no breakdown is provided of how many sequences survive each filter stage or the overall hit rate per filter level. No comparison is made between GenSLM and amino-acid-level PLMs (such as ESM-2) generating protein sequences at the same scale, leaving it unclear whether the codon-level approach specifically outperforms amino-acid-level generation, or whether any large-scale generation would achieve similar results. No structural data (crystal structures or cryo-EM) for GenSLM-230 or other active generated variants are provided, leaving the molecular basis for enhanced activity uncharacterized. No conformational dynamics analysis is presented — the connection between GenSLM-230's sequence and its functional superiority is entirely a "black box" at the molecular level. Finally, the reward signal for future iterative improvement of GenSLM is not defined: what quantitative metrics should GenSLM optimize in subsequent rounds?
