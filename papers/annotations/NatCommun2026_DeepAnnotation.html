Sequence-based generative AI design of versatile tryptophan synthases

ABS Abstract
ABS

Enzymes are powerful and sustainable catalysts, but their widespread application is limited by the difficulty of identifying functional starting points for optimization, creating a major bottleneck in early-stage biocatalyst discovery. Designing libraries of such starting enzymes remains particularly challenging. Here, we use the GenSLM protein language model to generate novel β-subunit of tryptophan synthase (TrpB) enzymes that express in Escherichia coli and are both stable and catalytically active. Many generated TrpBs also display significant substrate promiscuity, outperforming their natural counterparts on non-native substrates. Some even surpass laboratory-evolved TrpBs. Comparison of the most-active and most-promiscuous generated TrpB to its closest natural homolog confirms that the enhanced versatility is absent from the natural enzyme, highlighting the creative potential of generative models. These results demonstrate that the generated TrpBs not only preserve natural structure and function but also acquire non-natural properties, establishing generative models as powerful tools for biocatalyst discovery and engineering.

§1 Introduction — P1–P3
P1

Enzymes are exceptionally powerful, selective, and versatile catalysts for efficient and sustainable production of chemicals, fuels, materials, and pharmaceuticals, offering attractive alternatives to traditional chemical methods. However, to meet the performance requirements of industrial applications, enzymes often require tailored optimization. In this context, directed evolution (DE) has emerged as a robust and broadly applicable strategy, using iterative rounds of mutagenesis and screening to progressively improve an enzyme toward a desired function. Its success in producing industrially viable biocatalysts has been well documented, and recent advances in AI-guided design and laboratory automation can further streamline the DE workflow, significantly accelerating the pace and efficiency of enzyme engineering.

P2

Despite its broad applicability, directed evolution (DE) remains fundamentally limited by the need for a starting enzyme with measurable activity toward the target function. Identifying an enzyme with such initial activity is still largely empirical, with no universal strategy currently available. One common approach is to repurpose an existing enzyme by exploiting its ability to catalyze reactions or accept substrates beyond its native biological context, a property known as catalytic or substrate promiscuity. This typically begins by hypothesizing enzyme families that could accommodate the desired transformation based on mechanistic or substrate similarities. Libraries of enzymes are then constructed either by sampling natural sequence diversity or by creating mutant libraries from a small subset of representative enzymes. Both sampling natural sequences and mutating a given enzyme present challenges: natural enzymes may express poorly or have narrow specificity, while mutagenesis covers only a restricted sequence space and frequently yields a high proportion of inactive variants. The process is labor-intensive and time-consuming, with outcomes largely dictated by chance and the composition of the available enzyme libraries.

P3

To address the challenge of identifying novel enzymes with desired functions, we propose to use protein language models (PLMs) to generate libraries of enzymes that can be screened for target activities. PLM-generated proteins offer significant advantages over natural ones: they can explore regions of sequence space far from known proteins, while also allowing conditioning or filtering to incorporate desirable features. Recent experimental applications have validated this approach: PLMs can generate functional proteins with real-world relevance, marking a turning point for machine learning in protein engineering. Building on this promise, we used the GenSLM model, originally developed for genome-scale applications. Unlike most protein language models, which are trained on amino acid sequences, GenSLM learns interactions within DNA sequences at the codon level. However, experimental validation of DNA-sequence-based models has so far been limited and remains an open area for exploration.

§2 Methods — P4
P4

To assess the model's generative potential, we selected a mechanistically challenging enzyme as a test case. We focused on the β-subunit of tryptophan synthase (TrpB), a subunit of the heterotetrameric tryptophan synthase complex. TrpB catalyzes the condensation of indole with L-serine to form L-tryptophan, a reaction catalyzed by a bound PLP (pyridoxal-5-phosphate) cofactor. The enzyme is a key component in amino acid biosynthesis and has already been successfully engineered for novel biocatalytic functions. We used the GenSLM model to generate ~105 TrpB sequences, and then employed computational filtering to select for expression-promoting features (e.g., codon usage, avoiding destabilizing motifs). The generated sequences were then synthesized and expressed in E. coli, and screened for catalytic activity with L-serine and indole as substrates.

§3 Results — P5–P10
P5

A large fraction of the generated TrpBs successfully expressed in E. coli and folded into soluble protein. Nine had 80−90% sequence identity to natural TrpBs, and two had 70−80% sequence identity to a natural TrpB. This indicates that the model can generate sequences that are distant from natural proteins while still maintaining the ability to express and fold. At room temperature, a significant number of the candidates showed measurable indole and L-serine condensation activity, with several producing yields in the background. At 75 °C, seven GenSLM-TrpBs retained substantial activity, despite thermostability not being an explicit design criterion, with five of these having 80−90% and two having 70−80% sequence identity to the closest natural TrpB.

P6

Remarkably, several GenSLM-TrpBs exhibited levels of activity comparable to or even exceeding that of PfTrpB-0B2, a variant of PfTrpB specifically evolved for stand-alone function at 75 °C. Given that wild-type TrpBs require activation by their TrpA subunit, the high activity of the GenSLM-TrpBs, generated without consideration of TrpA, is noteworthy. It suggests that the model can generate sequences with properties typically acquired only through extensive laboratory evolution. Among these, 230 stands out: its total activity surpasses that of PfTrpB-0B2 at both room temperature and 75 °C. This striking result establishes that a GenSLM-designed TrpB can not only rival but even surpass a laboratory-evolved benchmark enzyme.

P7

To better understand the quality of the generated sequences, we performed multiple sequence alignment (MSA) of the GenSLM-TrpBs together with natural TrpBs, and examined their sequence logos. The analysis revealed that the generated sequences recapitulate the conserved regions and motifs observed in natural TrpBs, even though they diverge in more variable regions. This suggests that GenSLM learned the structural and functional constraints of the TrpB fold despite operating at the DNA sequence level. We also calculated conservation scores for individual positions and found that positions known to be critical for catalysis (e.g., the PLP-binding pocket) are highly conserved in GenSLM-TrpBs, similar to natural enzymes. These results indicate that the model has captured the essential biochemical requirements for function.

P8

Expression levels of the most active GenSLM-TrpBs were high, with yields ranging from several hundred milligrams to over a gram of purified protein per liter of culture. Melting temperatures (Tm) measured by thermoflour assay of the highest-performing GenSLM-TrpBs ranged from approximately 55 to 75 °C. These values are comparable to or exceed those of the wild-type TrpB and PfTrpB-0B2. The high expression levels and favorable thermostability, achieved without explicit optimization for these properties, further demonstrate the generative model's ability to produce practical biocatalysts.

P9

A striking feature of many GenSLM-TrpBs is their enhanced substrate promiscuity. We tested the generated enzymes with a range of non-native substrates, including D-serine, various aromatic aldehydes, and other amino acids. Many GenSLM-TrpBs showed higher activity toward these non-native substrates compared to natural TrpBs and some even surpassed PfTrpB-0B2. For example, with D-serine as the non-native amino acid, several GenSLM-TrpBs achieved 60-80% of their L-serine activity, far exceeding the performance of wild-type TrpB. This surprising result demonstrates that the generative model can discover sequences with genuinely novel catalytic properties not found in nature.

P10

Comparison of the most-active and most-promiscuous GenSLM-TrpBs to their closest natural homologs revealed striking differences in their catalytic properties. GenSLM-230, the top variant by combined activity metrics, shares approximately 85% sequence identity with the closest natural TrpB, yet exhibits substantially higher activity and broader substrate range. Introducing the sequence changes from GenSLM-230 into the natural enzyme background did not fully recapitulate the performance, suggesting that the improvements arise from a constellation of sequence changes that work synergistically, not from a single amino acid change. This underscores the value of generative models in exploring epistatic landscapes where combinations of mutations have non-additive effects.

§4 Discussion — P11–P12
P11

These results collectively demonstrate the creative potential of generative models for enzyme design and biocatalyst discovery. The ability to generate sequences that maintain natural-like structure and function while acquiring entirely novel catalytic properties—such as enhanced substrate promiscuity and activity on non-native substrates—opens new possibilities for tailored biocatalysis. Generative models can efficiently explore regions of sequence space that natural evolution and laboratory engineering have not yet accessed, providing a powerful complement or alternative to traditional directed evolution approaches. The success of DNA-sequence-based models like GenSLM, despite their limited prior experimental validation, suggests that the fundamental principles of protein function and structure are partially encoded in codon-level information. This finding has profound implications for future applications in protein engineering.

P11

P12

The work described here represents an important step in establishing generative models as a practical tool for industrial-scale biocatalyst discovery. By systematically generating, expressing, and characterizing large numbers of sequences, we have shown that generative models can accelerate the pace of enzyme engineering and reduce the reliance on traditional directed evolution. However, questions remain: How can we more efficiently design for specific functional properties? How can we improve the fraction of generated sequences that express and fold correctly? And how do we integrate computational predictions more tightly with experimental validation? Future work will likely combine generative models with high-throughput screening, machine learning-guided directed evolution, and physics-based simulations to create a fully integrated design-build-test-learn cycle. Such integrated approaches promise to dramatically expand the biocatalytic toolkit available to industry.

Protein Language Model (PLM) 蛋白质语言模型 用大规模序列数据预训练，学习蛋白质序列统计规律的神经网络 论文所属的广泛技术类别
TrpB 色氨酸合酶β亚基 催化吲哚 + L-丝氨酸 → L-色氨酸 的 PLP 依赖酶 论文的研究对象和测试案例
PLP 吡哆醛-5-磷酸 维生素 B6 衍生的辅酶，众多氨基酸代谢酶的活性中心 TrpB 的结合辅酶；保守位点检验的关键
Directed Evolution (DE) 定向进化 通过迭代突变和功能性筛选改进酶的经典方法 论文对标的传统方法；GenSLM-230 超越 DE 基准
Substrate promiscuity 底物通用性 酶接受和催化多种非天然底物的能力 生成 TrpB 的关键新性能；对 D-丝氨酸的活性
GenSLM-230 GenSLM 优选变体 论文中表现最突出的生成 TrpB，活性和通用性均超越 PfTrpB-0B2 论文的明星结果；你项目的首要 MetaDynamics 候选
PfTrpB-0B2 古细菌 TrpB 进化变体 Arnold 实验室通过定向进化的 PfTrpB 变体，含 6 个远端突变，优化为独立活性 论文的性能基准；某些生成变体超过它
COMM domain 通信结构域 TrpB 中进行 O↔PC↔C 构象转换的区域（残基 97-184） 虽然本文未显式讨论，但你的 MetaDynamics 关注的分子机制
