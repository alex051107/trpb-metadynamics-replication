ABS Abstract — GRPO Paper
G-ABS

Protein language models (PLMs) have achieved remarkable success in sequence modeling through large-scale supervised pretraining. However, this training paradigm fundamentally constrains the model to distributions observed in natural evolutionary history. The central question addressed in this work: can reinforcement learning push protein language models to discover sequence-structure-function relationships that lie beyond the coverage of natural protein databases? The authors introduce a framework applying Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to PLM fine-tuning for protein design, framing sequence generation as a reinforcement learning problem where the reward is a fitness signal from an external evaluator. The study systematically investigates four protein engineering tasks: antimicrobial peptide (AMP) generation, kinase variant optimization, antibody engineering, and inverse folding. A key conceptual contribution is the identification of a three-factor interaction model — task headroom, reward fidelity, and policy capacity — that jointly determines the magnitude of RL gains. The paper also introduces the Expansion-Shrinkage Ratio (ESR) metric to quantify whether RL training causes the model to expand into novel sequence space or collapse toward a narrow high-fitness mode.

§G1 Problem & Motivation
G-P1

Directed evolution (DE) remains the gold standard for protein engineering — but it is experimentally expensive and can only explore a small neighborhood around the parent sequence. The combinatorial space of a 200-residue protein is approximately 20200, making exhaustive search computationally and experimentally intractable. PLMs offer an alternative: sample from a learned distribution over sequences conditioned on function. But this immediately raises a fundamental problem. A PLM trained purely on natural sequences can only generate sequences whose fitness was "seen" by evolution — it cannot reliably extrapolate to engineered sequences with non-natural substrate specificity or enhanced catalytic activity. Furthermore, standard fine-tuning approaches (supervised fine-tuning on curated high-fitness sequences) require large labeled datasets that are typically unavailable for any specific protein engineering campaign. Reinforcement learning addresses this gap by replacing labeled pairs with a scalar reward signal: any fitness function — experimental assay, computational predictor, or physics simulation — can serve as the reward, enabling the model to explore beyond its pretraining prior without requiring a labeled dataset of optimal sequences.

§G2 GRPO Algorithm Core
G-P2

GRPO was originally proposed in DeepSeekMath for LLM mathematical reasoning. Its defining feature — the innovation that makes it practical for large protein language models — is the complete elimination of the critic/value network that is central to standard PPO. In PPO, a separate value network V(s) is trained in parallel to estimate expected returns and compute advantage estimates. This doubles the model parameters and GPU memory footprint. GRPO replaces this with a group-relative baseline: for each prompt (protein context), a group of G sequences is sampled from the current policy, scored, and the advantage of each sample is computed relative to the group mean. The training objective:

For each prompt context:
1. Sample G sequences {s₁, ..., s_G} from π_θ
2. Evaluate reward r_i for each s_i
3. Compute group advantage:
   A_i = (r_i - mean(r)) / (std(r) + ε)
4. Policy gradient loss:
   L = -E[min(A_i·ρ_i, A_i·clip(ρ_i, 1-ε, 1+ε))]
   + β · KL(π_θ ‖ π_ref)
where ρ_i = π_θ(s_i) / π_ref(s_i)

The KL penalty term with coefficient β prevents the policy from drifting too far from the reference model (typically the pretrained PLM), balancing exploration with preservation of the pretraining prior. Without KL regularization, RL training can cause catastrophic forgetting of the structural knowledge encoded during pretraining.

KL 惩罚的重要性：没有 β·KL 项，RL 训练会导致 catastrophic forgetting——PLM 丢失预训练中学到的蛋白质结构知识（例如疏水核心、二级结构偏好），生成根本不可折叠的序列。β 需要仔细调参：太小→探索过度；太大→几乎不更新。
与你的项目：对 GenSLM 的 GRPO 训练，G（每轮采样数）决定了每次迭代需要运行多少次 F1（MetaDynamics）评估。如果 G=16，每轮需要 16 次 MetaD 仿真——计算成本巨大。实践中应分级：G 个序列先跑 F0（PLACER），顶部几个才跑 F1。
§G3 Group Advantage A_i — 核心公式解析
G-P3

The group advantage A_i is the heart of GRPO's innovation and deserves careful analysis. For a group of G sequences sampled from the same protein context:

# Given rewards: r = [r_1, r_2, ..., r_G]
mean_r = sum(r) / G
std_r = sqrt(sum((r_i - mean_r)^2) / G)

# Group-relative advantage
A_i = (r_i - mean_r) / (std_r + epsilon)

# Properties:
# A_i > 0 → sequence i is above-average (reinforce)
# A_i < 0 → sequence i is below-average (suppress)
# A_i ≈ 0 → sequence i is median (minimal update)

This formulation has a crucial property: the baseline is purely empirical, computed from the same generation batch, with no additional neural network required. The absolute reward scale is irrelevant — only the relative ranking within the group matters. This means reward shaping and normalization are automatically handled by the group statistics. The epsilon term (typically 1e-8) prevents division by zero when all rewards are identical (a degenerate case where no learning signal is available).

段落功能：对 A_i 公式进行逐行解析，强调三个关键性质：① 不需要额外神经网络；② 只关注组内相对排名，不关注绝对奖励大小；③ 自动处理奖励归一化问题。

退化陷阱：如果所有 G 个序列的奖励都几乎相同（std_r ≈ 0），GRPO 产生几乎零梯度。这可能发生在 F0 奖励（PLACER）的辨别力很低时——所有序列得分相似。解决方案：使用更高分辨率的奖励函数，或者增大组大小 G。
与 PPO 的实质差异：PPO 的优势 A = r - V(s)，其中 V(s) 是价值网络对"未来期望收益"的预测。V(s) 需要被分开训练，且在 RL 早期往往不准确（导致高方差梯度）。GRPO 的组内均值是对同等条件下期望收益的无偏估计，统计上更可靠。
§G4 GRPO vs PPO vs DPO — 算法比较
G-P4

Three prominent RL/preference-alignment algorithms compete for the task of fine-tuning PLMs. Understanding their differences is essential for making the right algorithmic choice.

Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is the canonical on-policy RL algorithm. It requires a critic/value network trained in parallel, approximately doubling GPU memory. The clipped surrogate objective ensures stable training. PPO works well when the reward function is well-defined and the environment is stationary, but the value network adds significant engineering complexity.

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) sidesteps the RL formulation entirely. It learns directly from preference pairs (winner sequence w, loser sequence l), maximizing the log-ratio of their probabilities under the policy relative to the reference. The critical limitation: DPO requires preference pairs — binary comparisons — not scalar rewards. This means DPO cannot directly use physics-based fitness scores (e.g., MetaDynamics free energy values) without converting them to pairwise comparisons, which discards quantitative information.

GRPO's decisive advantage for protein engineering: it requires only scalar rewards, works with any fitness oracle, and eliminates the critic model — making it the practical choice when the reward is a computational simulation (slow, expensive) rather than a classifier.

§G5 奖励函数设计 — Reward Functions
G-P5

The reward function is the single most important design decision in any RL system. This paper tests four distinct reward formulations across its four tasks:

AMP Task: R(s) = 2·(f_MIC(s) − 0.4), where f_MIC is the binary probability output of the ApexMIC classifier predicting antimicrobial activity. The formula centers the reward at 0 when f_MIC = 0.4 (baseline), making R positive for above-baseline sequences and negative for below-baseline sequences. The factor of 2 scales the reward range to approximately [−0.8, +1.2].

Kinase Task: Direct experimentally measured fitness values from a published dataset, normalized to zero mean and unit variance before use as rewards.

Antibody Task: ΔΔG binding affinity predicted by ProtAttBA model — negative values indicate improved binding.

Inverse Folding: TM-score of the designed sequence's predicted structure against the target structure.

A consistent observation: the accuracy of the reward function is more predictive of RL success than the choice of algorithm. A noisy reward that incorrectly ranks sequences leads to RL updates that reinforce low-fitness sequences, ultimately degrading model quality regardless of the RL algorithm used.

§G6 ESR — Expansion-Shrinkage Ratio
G-P6

One of the paper's original contributions is the Expansion-Shrinkage Ratio (ESR), a diagnostic metric for understanding what RL training is doing to the model's sequence space coverage. The intuition: after RL training, some sequence regions become more likely (expanded) while others become less likely (shrunk). ESR quantifies this change at the token/amino acid level.

Formally, for each amino acid position and residue type, compare the sampling probability before and after RL training. ESR is defined as the ratio of the fraction of amino acid/token positions whose probability increased (expanded) to the fraction whose probability decreased (shrunk).

Key empirical results:

AMP task: ESR = 0.14 — only 14% of the change was expansion, 86% was shrinkage. The model focused tightly on high-antimicrobial-activity regions, discarding most of the pretraining diversity. This is good for the AMP task (we want AMP-specific sequences) but means the model lost generality.

Kinase task: ESR = 0.08 — extreme focus on the small high-fitness kinase region. Very high exploitation, very low exploration.

Antibody task: ESR = 0.50 — mixed, indicating the antibody RL training both expanded and shrunk different regions approximately equally. This task showed the worst RL outcomes, consistent with the view that mixed ESR reflects confused/conflicting reward signals.

ESR close to 0 = surgical focus on a narrow high-reward region (risk: diversity collapse). ESR close to 1 = balanced exploration-exploitation. ESR >> 1 = the model is expanding into novel territory (risk: reward hacking).

§G7 三因子交互模型 — Headroom × Fidelity × Capacity
G-P7

The paper's central theoretical contribution is identifying three factors that jointly determine RL gains:

Factor 1 — Task Headroom: How much improvement is possible beyond the supervised pretraining baseline? If the PLM already generates near-optimal sequences (high baseline fitness), RL has little room to improve. Conversely, if the task requires properties far from natural sequences (e.g., non-natural substrate specificity), the RL improvement potential is high. Operationally measured as: mean fitness of RL-generated sequences minus mean fitness of PLM-baseline sequences, normalized by the theoretical maximum improvement.

Factor 2 — Reward Fidelity: How accurately does the reward function rank sequences? A perfectly correlated reward (rank correlation = 1.0 between predicted and true fitness) maximizes learning signal. A noisy reward (random ranking) provides no signal and may actively mislead. The paper demonstrates that RL gains plateau and reverse when reward fidelity drops below approximately 0.4 Spearman correlation.

Factor 3 — Policy Capacity: Is the model large enough to learn the new patterns required by the task? Small PLMs hit hard capacity limits — they can improve on simple tasks but cannot learn complex new sequence-function relationships. Larger models benefit more from RL, assuming sufficient headroom and fidelity.

RL gain ≈ f(headroom) × g(fidelity) × h(capacity) — the three factors are multiplicative, not additive. A single bottleneck factor can nullify gains from the other two.

§G8 实验结果 — 跨任务数据
G-P8

Quantitative results across four tasks provide empirical grounding for the three-factor model:

AMP design: GRPO outperformed both PPO and DPO on the percentage of generated sequences passing the ApexMIC antimicrobial threshold (72% vs 58% PPO vs 51% DPO). ESR = 0.14, confirming focused exploration. The reward signal (f_MIC classifier) has moderate fidelity — yet RL still produced clear gains, consistent with high headroom for AMP (many unnatural AMP sequences exist outside the training distribution).

Kinase variant optimization: ESR = 0.08 — the smallest value observed, indicating extreme mode collapse toward high-fitness kinase sequences. This is acceptable for kinase optimization (we want kinase-specific sequences), but diversity dropped dramatically, which would be problematic if we wanted to maintain functional diversity or avoid overfitting to a specific assay.

Antibody engineering: ESR = 0.50, mixed RL outcomes. This task showed the weakest RL gains despite high policy capacity. Retrospective analysis suggests the antibody task had lower headroom (the PLM baseline already generates reasonable antibodies) and the ΔΔG reward signal has higher noise for sequences far outside the antibody training distribution.

Inverse folding: TM-score improved by approximately +0.08 on held-out test structures. High reward fidelity (TM-score is a robust, widely-validated metric) contributed to strong RL gains.

§G9 迭代精化 — Multi-Round RL Training
G-P9

A critical but often overlooked aspect of RL fine-tuning is that it is not a one-shot procedure. The reference policy π_ref must be periodically refreshed to prevent the KL penalty from becoming an increasingly tight leash as the policy diverges over many training steps.

The iterative refinement protocol:

Round 0: π_ref = pretrained PLM
Round 1: GRPO train → π_1; check ESR, fitness
  → update: π_ref = π_1
Round 2: GRPO train → π_2; check ESR, fitness
  → update: π_ref = π_2
...
Stop when: ESR < threshold OR fitness plateaus

The reference update serves two purposes: (1) it allows the KL constraint to "relax" around the new, better policy, enabling further improvement; (2) it uses the improved policy as a starting point for the next round, compounding gains. Without reference updates, the KL penalty would eventually prevent any further RL improvement, regardless of how much headroom remains.

Risk of over-refreshing: if π_ref is updated too frequently (every few steps), the KL constraint becomes ineffective and the policy can drift catastrophically. Typical refresh schedule: every 500–2000 gradient steps.

Directed evolution is limited by the experimental throughput available to any single lab. Even with robotics, exhaustive screening of a 160,000-variant combinatorial library is infeasible. The BO-EVO framework addresses this by treating protein fitness optimization as a Bayesian optimization (BO) problem: instead of random or heuristic screening, a probabilistic surrogate model (Gaussian Process) predicts fitness across the sequence space with uncertainty estimates, and an acquisition function (UCB) strategically selects the most informative sequences to evaluate next. The evolutionary algorithm component generates biologically plausible candidate sequences via single-point mutations, restricting search to the functional neighborhood. Applied to the RhlA enzyme, the framework achieves 4.8× improvement in rhamnolipid production specificity within 4 rounds of 384-variant batches — evaluating less than 1% of the theoretical sequence space. The paper extends this to a multi-fidelity (MFBO) formulation where evaluations at different cost/accuracy tiers are coordinated by a cost-normalized acquisition function: acqf_MF = acqf / cost(l), systematically routing sequences to the appropriate evaluation tier based on predicted benefit per unit cost.

§M1 Problem & Motivation — 蛋白质工程的组合爆炸
M-P1

Protein engineering faces a fundamental combinatorial challenge: a protein with n mutable positions and 20 amino acid choices has 20n possible variants. For n=5 positions, this is 3.2 million variants; for n=10, 10 trillion. Even with robotic high-throughput screening capable of testing 10,000 variants per day, sampling n=10 positions exhaustively would take 2,700 years. Standard directed evolution addresses this by limiting exploration to single or double mutations per round, focusing on local optima rather than global optima. The central open problem: can we identify high-fitness variants in the global sequence space using only 1% of the experimental budget?

The authors frame this as a sequential decision problem under uncertainty: at each round, given the fitness data from previous rounds, which sequences should we evaluate next to maximize the expected improvement in our best variant? This framing immediately suggests Bayesian optimization — the mathematical framework specifically designed for sequential decision-making under uncertainty with expensive function evaluations.

The key insight that distinguishes this work: a surrogate model trained on ESM-1v sequence embeddings can generalize across the sequence space, predicting fitness for sequences that have never been experimentally evaluated. This generalization power is what allows BO to guide search far beyond the local mutation neighborhood explored by standard DE.

§M2 GP 代理模型 — Gaussian Process Surrogate
M-P2

The Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) surrogate model is the probabilistic backbone of BO-EVO. For a set of evaluated sequences X = {x_1, ..., x_n} with measured fitness y = {y_1, ..., y_n}, the GP models the fitness function as a random process with a prior over functions defined by a kernel (covariance function):

# GP prior: f ~ GP(μ_0, k)
# After observing data (X, y):
f(x*) | X, y ~ N(μ(x*), σ²(x*))

μ(x*) = K(x*,X) · [K(X,X)+σ²I]⁻¹ · y
σ²(x*) = k(x*,x*) - K(x*,X)·[K(X,X)+σ²I]⁻¹·K(X,x*)

The kernel k(x, x') = exp(−‖φ(x)−φ(x')‖² / 2l²) where φ(x) is the ESM-1v embedding of sequence x and l is the length scale hyperparameter. This RBF kernel measures sequence similarity in the learned embedding space. The key properties of GP posterior: μ(x*) is the best prediction of fitness for unobserved sequence x*; σ(x*) quantifies our uncertainty — high σ means we have little data near x* in the embedding space. GP inference scales as O(N³) in the number of data points N — with thousands of evaluations, sparse GP approximations become necessary.

§M3 UCB 采集函数 — 探索与利用的平衡
M-P3

The acquisition function determines which sequences to evaluate next. Among all BO acquisition functions, Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) provides the clearest exploration-exploitation tradeoff:

UCB(x) = μ(x) + κ · σ(x)

# κ = 0.0: pure exploitation (always pick highest μ)
# κ → ∞: pure exploration (always pick highest σ)
# κ = 2.0: typical balanced value

Select: x* = argmax_x UCB(x)
(over evolutionary algorithm candidate set)

The term κ·σ(x) is often called the "optimism bonus" — it gives extra value to sequences in unexplored regions of sequence space, ensuring the algorithm doesn't prematurely converge to a local optimum. UCB is "optimistic in the face of uncertainty": if we're uncertain about a sequence's fitness, we optimistically assume it might be very good and invest in finding out.

UCB's key limitation: it requires the GP uncertainty to be well-calibrated. If σ(x) is systematically underestimated (overconfident GP), UCB collapses to pure exploitation. If σ(x) is overestimated (underconfident GP), UCB wastes evaluations on low-fitness unexplored regions. Calibration checks (comparing predicted σ to empirical residuals) should be part of the BO pipeline.

§M4 进化算法集成 — 候选序列生成
M-P4

A pure BO approach would need to evaluate UCB over all possible sequences — computationally intractable for the full 20n space. BO-EVO's solution: use an evolutionary algorithm as a candidate generator, restricting the UCB optimization to biologically plausible sequences reachable by mutation from a parent sequence.

The evolutionary algorithm component:

1. Start from a parent sequence (highest fitness in current data, or initial parent)

2. Generate all single-point mutations (or a random subset): systematically substitute each position with all 20 amino acids to create a pool of ~20n candidates

3. Score all candidates via the GP surrogate: compute UCB(x) for each

4. Select the top-B candidates by UCB score as the next evaluation batch (B = 384 in the paper)

5. Evaluate the selected B sequences experimentally (or computationally)

6. Update the GP with new data: re-condition the GP on all observed (x, y) pairs

Repeat for R rounds. This single-point mutation strategy achieves two goals simultaneously: it ensures generated sequences are biologically plausible (within one mutation of a known functional sequence) and it restricts the UCB search space to O(20n) rather than 20n, making GP-UCB optimization tractable.

§M5 多保真度理论 — Multi-Fidelity Framework
M-P5

Standard BO evaluates all candidates at the same fidelity level. Multi-Fidelity BO (MFBO) introduces a hierarchy of evaluators at different cost-accuracy tradeoffs. The key observation: many cheap low-fidelity evaluations can collectively provide information comparable to one expensive high-fidelity evaluation, if the fidelity levels are correlated.

The MFBO framework requires three components:

1. Multi-fidelity GP kernel: The GP kernel is extended to include a fidelity dimension, allowing information from cheap evaluations to inform predictions at expensive fidelity levels:

k((x,l), (x',l')) = k_input(x,x') × k_fidelity(l,l')

# k_input: sequence similarity (ESM-1v kernel)
# k_fidelity: correlation between fidelity levels
# l=0 (F0, fast), l=1 (F1, medium), l=2 (F2, expensive)

2. Cost model: Each fidelity level has an associated cost. In the TrpB context: cost(F0) ~ seconds per sequence; cost(F1) ~ hours per sequence (MetaD run); cost(F2) ~ days per sequence (EVB/QM-MM).

3. Cost-normalized acquisition: See P6.

The multi-fidelity GP exploits cross-fidelity correlations: if F0 predicts that sequence X is a top candidate, and the F0-to-F1 correlation is 0.7, then the GP can update its F1 prediction for X even before running any F1 evaluation. This information transfer reduces the number of expensive F1/F2 evaluations needed.

§M6 多保真度采集函数 — acqf_MF = acqf / cost(l)
M-P6

The heart of MFBO is the cost-normalized acquisition function, which makes decisions about both which sequence to evaluate and at which fidelity level:

acqf_MF(x, l) = acqf_standard(x, l) / cost(l)

# acqf_standard: e.g., UCB, EI, PI at fidelity l
# cost(l): computational/experimental cost of fidelity l

Decision: (x*, l*) = argmax_{x,l} acqf_MF(x, l)

# Example TrpB costs:
cost(F0) = 1 # seconds → normalized to 1
cost(F1) = 3600 # ~1 hour MetaD run
cost(F2) = 86400 # ~1 day EVB calculation

Interpretation: acqf_MF measures "information gain per unit computational cost". A medium-fidelity evaluation that provides 80% of the information at 10% of the cost of high-fidelity will be preferred until the marginal gain of high-fidelity justifies its cost.

The algorithm automatically routes sequences to F0 for initial screening, promotes borderline candidates to F1 when F0 uncertainty is high, and only commits to F2 when F1 data confirms a candidate is likely near-optimal. This creates the natural "funnel" shape characteristic of efficient screening campaigns.

Critical caveat: the cost model must be accurate. If cost(F1) is underestimated (e.g., a MetaD run takes 3 days instead of 1 hour), the algorithm will over-invest in F1 evaluations, defeating the purpose.

M-P8

The Ramanathan Lab's F0/F1/F2 evaluation tiers directly instantiate the multi-fidelity framework. Each tier corresponds to an evaluator with distinct cost, accuracy, and information content:

F0 — PLACER Sequence Scoring: Fast sequence-level predictor (seconds per sequence). Evaluates amino acid composition, evolutionary conservation, physicochemical properties. Cannot assess conformational dynamics or substrate specificity directly. Serves as the initial funnel: screen thousands of GenSLM sequences, eliminate obvious non-starters.

F1 — MetaDynamics + ΔΔG: Physics-based conformational and binding evaluation (hours per sequence). MetaDynamics FEL provides the COMM domain O→C transition probability — the key indicator of whether the protein can adopt the catalytically active conformation. ΔΔG binding energy assesses substrate affinity. This is the authoritative fitness signal for GRPO training: it encodes whether a sequence has the right conformational dynamics for D-Trp catalysis.

F2 — EVB / QM-MM: Quantum chemistry-level catalytic barrier calculation (days per sequence). The gold standard for confirming catalytic activity. Only the top 1–5% of F1 candidates warrant F2 evaluation.

The pipeline: GenSLM samples G sequences → all G sequences evaluated at F0 → MFBO selects top subset for F1 → F1 results used as GRPO reward to update GenSLM → MFBO selects top F1 candidates for F2 → F2 validates final candidates for synthesis.

§C 开放问题 — 尚未解决

§C · 五个核心开放问题

1. 奖励归一化：F0 + F1 如何合并？

GRPO 需要单一标量奖励。如果同时使用 F0 和 F1 评估，需要合并为一个 reward：

r_i = α · r_F0_i + (1-α) · r_F1_i

问题：α 应该取什么值？如何随训练进展动态调整 α？

三因子模型的建议：α 应该随 F0-F1 Spearman 相关性 ρ 调整。如果 ρ < 0.4，令 α → 0（完全用 F1）。如果 ρ > 0.7，可以令 α = 0.3（F0 作为辅助信号加速训练）。先验证 ρ 再决定 α。

2. ESR 的具体计算协议

论文给出了 ESR 的定义（扩展 token 占比 / 收缩 token 占比），但没有给出 GenSLM codon-level token space 的具体实现。GenSLM 的输入可能是密码子而不是氨基酸——ESR 的计算是在密码子层面还是氨基酸层面？

建议：在氨基酸层面计算 ESR（用 GenSLM 输出概率解码到氨基酸），这样与 GRPO 论文的定义保持一致，也更易于与序列多样性指标对比。密码子层面的 ESR 会高估真实序列多样性变化（同义突变会贡献 ESR 但不改变蛋白质序列）。

3. 稀疏 GP 的必要性阈值

精确 GP 在 N >~2000 时计算成本过高（O(N³)）。对 TrpB pipeline，F0 评估可能累积数千条记录。何时需要切换到稀疏 GP（SVGP）？诱导点数量 M 应该取多少？

实践建议：N < 2000 用精确 GP；N = 2000–10000 用稀疏 GP，M = min(500, N/4)；N > 10000 考虑 Deep Kernel Learning（DKL）或神经过程替代。BoTorch 的 ApproximateGP 实现稀疏 GP，接口与精确 GP 相同，切换成本低。

4. F0 对 GenSLM 序列的准确性验证

PLACER 在自然 TrpB 序列上训练，可能对 GenSLM 生成的非自然序列（远离自然分布）准确性很差。这是三因子模型"奖励保真度"瓶颈的核心风险。

必做实验：收集第一批 F1 评估数据（~30 个序列）后，立即计算 PLACER F0 分数与 MetaD F1 分数的 Spearman 相关系数。如果 ρ < 0.4，立即停止使用 F0 作为 RL 奖励；如果 ρ > 0.6，可以在早期训练轮次中使用 F0 加速探索。

5. Single-walker MetaD 作为 F1 的噪声水平

当前 Job 41514529 是 single-walker MetaD。Single-walker FEL 存在收敛噪声（同一序列跑两次可能给出 ±2–3 kcal/mol 的壁垒差异）。对 GRPO 来说，奖励噪声会降低三因子的"保真度"因子。多少噪声是可接受的？

理论分析：GRPO 的组内归一化（A_i = (r-μ)/σ）对奖励噪声有一定容忍度。如果噪声标准差 δ 远小于组内 reward 的真实标准差 σ_true，则 SNR = σ_true / δ > 1，RL 仍然有效。建议：同一序列跑 2 次 single-walker MetaD，比较壁垒估计差异（δ）；估计不同序列间的 reward 差异范围（σ_true）；确保 SNR > 2 才用 single-walker 作为 F1 奖励。
