ABS Abstract
ABS

The calculation of free energy surfaces by metadynamics requires the prior definition of collective variables (CVs) that must capture the slow degrees of freedom of the system. Here we present Deep-TDA, a method that combines targeted discriminant analysis (TDA) with deep learning to construct data-driven CVs that optimally discriminate between user-defined metastable states. A key challenge is the presence of hidden intermediate states that are unknown a priori but critical for convergence. We address this by combining Deep-TDA with metadynamics of paths (MoP), which explores the space of reactive trajectories rather than configuration space, enabling the automatic discovery of previously unknown intermediate states. The iterative workflow — train initial CV, run MoP to discover intermediates, retrain n-state CV, run production MetaD — is validated on a 2D model potential (Müller-Brown, 120 kBT barrier) and alanine dipeptide at 300 K. In both cases, the refined multi-state CV achieves accuracy matching or exceeding hand-crafted CVs while requiring no prior knowledge of intermediates. Deep-TDA is implemented as a PLUMED module using libtorch for neural network inference.

§1 Introduction — P1–P3
P1

The accurate computation of free energy surfaces (FES) is one of the central challenges in computational chemistry and biophysics. Molecular dynamics simulations can in principle sample the thermodynamically relevant regions of conformational space, but in practice the presence of high free energy barriers — often many times kBT — means that transitions between metastable states are extremely rare on the timescales accessible to straightforward simulation. Enhanced sampling methods such as umbrella sampling, replica exchange, and metadynamics are designed to overcome this limitation by artificially driving the system over these barriers. However, the efficiency of all such methods depends critically on the choice of collective variables (CVs): low-dimensional functions of the atomic coordinates that parameterize the slow degrees of freedom of the system. Choosing a poor CV — one that fails to capture all relevant slow motions — leads to hidden barriers, slow convergence, and systematic errors in the computed FES. The central problem is that identifying good CVs typically requires substantial prior knowledge about the system, creating a circular dependency: good CVs require knowing the important states, but finding the important states requires good CVs.

P2

Several approaches have been proposed to alleviate the burden of CV design. Path collective variables (Branduardi et al.) parameterize the reaction coordinate as progress along a reference path connecting known end states, but require explicit knowledge of both endpoints and implicitly assume the transition proceeds through the provided path. If unknown intermediates exist off this reference path, the path CV will fail to sample them, leading to incomplete or biased FES. Data-driven approaches including time-lagged independent component analysis (TICA), variational autoencoders (VAE), and SGOOP have been proposed to extract CVs from simulation data without prior knowledge, but they require pre-existing simulation data covering the full conformational space — which brings back the original problem. Machine learning methods such as DeepLDA and DeepTICA can learn nonlinear CVs, but rely on labeled configurations from known states, limiting their ability to discover new states. None of these approaches provides a principled way to automatically discover unknown intermediate metastable states and incorporate them into the CV without user intervention.

P3

Here we present Deep Targeted Discriminant Analysis (Deep-TDA), a method that addresses both the CV learning and the intermediate state discovery problems within a unified iterative framework. Deep-TDA is a supervised neural network trained to discriminate between user-specified metastable states, learning a nonlinear one-dimensional projection that maximally separates the states while clustering configurations within each state. The key innovation is the integration of Deep-TDA with Metadynamics of Paths (MoP), a recently introduced method that operates in the space of reactive trajectories rather than configuration space. When the initial 2-state Deep-TDA CV is used to drive MoP simulations, the reactive trajectories spontaneously detour through hidden intermediate basins — these detours are then clustered to identify new intermediate states, which are incorporated into a refined n-state Deep-TDA CV. This iterative process continues until the system achieves full A-to-B transitions, at which point the final CV is used for production metadynamics or OPES to compute the converged FES. The complete method requires only endpoint configuration data (unbiased MD from A and B) and produces, as output, a converged FES with all relevant intermediate states automatically identified.

§2 Methods — P4–P7
P4

The Deep-TDA neural network is a feed-forward network with 3 hidden layers of {24, 12, 1} nodes, using ReLU activation functions and a 1-dimensional output representing the learned CV value s(R). The network is trained with the ADAM optimizer at a learning rate of 10−3, with L2 regularization coefficient λ = 10−5 to prevent overfitting. Training is stopped by early stopping with patience = 15 epochs. The input features to the network are typically distances, angles, or other structural descriptors computed from atomic coordinates. The compact architecture (24-12-1) is intentionally simple: it provides sufficient nonlinearity to learn discriminant projections while remaining fast enough for on-the-fly inference during PLUMED-driven MD simulations. The network is implemented and exported using PyTorch (TorchScript), and called at each MD step via PLUMED's PYTORCH_MODEL collective variable, which links to the libtorch C++ backend. This design allows the network to be trained in Python and deployed in the GROMACS+PLUMED pipeline without recompiling the MD engine.

P5

The TDA loss function is designed to simultaneously minimize intra-state variance and maximize inter-state separation in the learned 1D CV space. Specifically, the loss is:

L = α · Σi Var(s(R) | state i) − β · Σi≠j (μi − μj)2

where μi is the mean CV value for state i, Var(s | state i) is the variance of the CV within state i, and α = 1, β = 250 are hyperparameters controlling the relative weight of the two terms. The first term penalizes spread within each state (configurations of the same state should map to similar CV values). The second term rewards separation between states (different states should have maximally different mean CV values). The asymmetry α/β = 1/250 means the inter-state separation term dominates, which is physically motivated: good CVs for metadynamics need to clearly identify which basin a configuration belongs to. For multi-state CVs (n > 2), the loss naturally extends to penalize all pairwise within-state variances and reward all pairwise inter-state separations, ensuring each state gets its own characteristic region of the 1D CV axis.

与线性LDA的关系：如果把神经网络换成单层线性映射，TDA loss就退化为经典Fisher Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)。Deep-TDA = 非线性版LDA，通过神经网络获得更强的判别能力。这也是命名"Deep TDA"而非"DeepLDA"的原因（TDA特指这种特定损失形式）。
实现细节：用PyTorch实现loss很直接：
loss = alpha * sum(var_within) - beta * sum(dist_between)
其中var_within按state分组计算，dist_between计算所有state对的均值差平方。
P6

Metadynamics of Paths (MoP) is a method that operates in the space of reactive trajectories rather than individual configurations. The central insight is a mathematical isomorphism between the distribution of reactive paths and the Boltzmann distribution of an elastic polymer chain: a trajectory {R1, R2, ..., RN} of N configurations is treated as a "polymer" with beads at each configuration, connected by harmonic springs. The trajectory CV is defined as S({Rn}) = s(RN) − s(R1), measuring the end-to-end progress of the trajectory in Deep-TDA CV space. Metadynamics bias is applied to this trajectory CV, driving the polymer to explore a wide variety of path shapes — including those that detour through hidden intermediate basins. Crucially, trajectories where |S| is small (the polymer is "crumpled") correspond to reactive paths that do not progress along the known CV direction — these are the signatures of unknown intermediate states pulling the trajectory off the direct A→B route. For the alanine dipeptide benchmark, the MoP simulation used N = 512 polymer beads, 4 × 106 steps, timestep 0.5 fs (config) / 1.0 fs (trajectory), and Langevin damping 500 fs / 1,000 fs for configuration and trajectory spaces respectively.

P7

The complete iterative Deep-TDA protocol proceeds as follows: (Step 1) Run short unbiased MD simulations starting from the known endpoint states A and B, collecting 4,000 configurations per state as training data. (Step 2) Train the initial 2-state Deep-TDA CV on the A and B configurations using the TDA loss. (Step 3) Run MoP using this initial CV as the trajectory CV S. Collect the resulting reactive trajectory ensemble. (Step 4) Cluster the MoP trajectories using k-means or HDBSCAN in the space of the current CV to identify candidate intermediate state clusters. Visually inspect (or use automated criteria) to confirm whether new metastable basins have been discovered. (Step 5) If new states B1, B2, ... are found, collect additional unbiased MD data from each new state and retrain as an n-state CV including all known states. (Step 6) Repeat from Step 3 until MoP shows complete A→B transitions without detours (i.e., the CV is complete). (Step 7) Use the final n-state CV for production metadynamics or OPES to compute the converged FES.

§3 Results — P8–P10
P8

Müller-Brown Potential Benchmark. The Müller-Brown 2D model potential provides a controlled test with three metastable states A, B, and C separated by barriers of up to 120 kBT — an extreme case designed to stress-test the method. The ground truth FES is analytically known. Starting from an initial 2-state CV trained on states A and C (the two endpoints), the initial Deep-TDA CV correctly separates A from C, but fails to identify the intermediate state B, which maps to a CV value overlapping with C. MoP applied with this initial CV spontaneously discovers the B basin via "crumpled" trajectories that detour through B. After clustering the MoP trajectories and collecting additional unbiased data from B, the CV is retrained as a 4-state CV (A, B, C, plus a transition state region). With this refined CV, the computed free energy difference converges to 12.15 kBT, in excellent agreement with the analytical value. Reactive trajectories sampled with the refined CV align precisely with the minimum free energy paths on the analytical surface, confirming the CV captures all relevant slow motions.

P9

Alanine Dipeptide Benchmark. The alanine dipeptide in explicit water at 300 K provides a more realistic molecular test case, with three conformational states C5, C7eq, and Cax defined by the φ and ψ backbone dihedral angles. This system has a well-established reference FES computed using explicit φ, ψ dihedrals as CVs, enabling quantitative validation. Starting from an initial 2-state Deep-TDA CV trained on C5 and C7eq (using 4,000 configurations per state), the initial CV shows a 0.2 kBT discrepancy compared to the reference dihedral method after 5 ns of biased simulation — this is due to the Cax intermediate being unaccounted for. After MoP discovers Cax and the CV is retrained as a 3-state CV, the FES matches the dihedral reference with no free parameter tuning, showing faster convergence (smaller statistical fluctuations at equivalent simulation time) and quantitative agreement within 0.2 kBT across all basins. Notably, the Deep-TDA input features used here are sin(φ), cos(φ), sin(ψ), cos(ψ) — just four descriptors, illustrating that the method can work with compact, physically meaningful input features as well as large descriptor sets.

A systematic comparison of Deep-TDA (refined n-state CV) against hand-crafted CVs demonstrates that the data-driven approach matches or exceeds the accuracy of expert-designed CVs while requiring no prior knowledge of intermediate states. For the Müller-Brown potential, the reference "hand-crafted" CV is a linear projection in (x, y) space that was specifically constructed to capture the A→B→C minimum free energy path — effectively an oracle CV. Deep-TDA achieves the same accuracy (ΔF = 12.15 kBT) after the iterative refinement. For alanine dipeptide, the explicit φ, ψ dihedral angles represent the physically motivated hand-crafted CV. Deep-TDA with 4-dimensional input achieves equivalent accuracy to this reference. In both cases, the key advantage of Deep-TDA is not higher accuracy but rather the ability to achieve this accuracy without specifying intermediate states a priori. The iterative MoP-based protocol substitutes for the human expert's prior knowledge of the conformational landscape. However, the authors note that if the endpoint states A and B are very similar in terms of input features (low separation), the initial 2-state CV may not be sufficiently discriminative to drive useful MoP exploration, representing a potential failure mode for systems with subtle conformational changes.

§4 Discussion & Limitations — P11
P11

Deep-TDA with iterative MoP is most effective when endpoint states are well-separated in the chosen input feature space and when the number of relevant intermediate states is modest. For systems with many intermediate states or complex multidimensional free energy surfaces, the current 1D CV output may be insufficient; in such cases, extending to a 2D or higher-dimensional output could be considered, though this increases the complexity of the MoP framework. The method is also sensitive to the quality of the training data: short unbiased MD simulations may not adequately sample the full intramolecular distribution within each endpoint state, leading to biased training sets. The authors recommend at minimum 50–100 ns of unbiased MD per endpoint state for molecular systems of the size of alanine dipeptide; for larger systems such as proteins, significantly longer simulations or clustering of pre-existing long MD trajectories may be necessary. The MoP computational cost scales as O(N) in the number of polymer beads N relative to ordinary MD, making MoP simulations of large proteins substantially more expensive than standard MetaD. For TrpB-scale systems (~40,000 atoms), MoP with N=512 beads would require roughly 512× the computational resources of an equivalent MetaD step — necessitating careful consideration of bead number vs. intermediate discovery fidelity.

§5 Conclusion — P12
P12

We have presented Deep-TDA, a method for learning data-driven collective variables for metadynamics via targeted discriminant analysis combined with iterative intermediate state discovery through metadynamics of paths. The key contributions are: (1) a loss function that directly optimizes CV quality for metadynamics convergence (TDA loss with α=1, β=250); (2) integration with MoP to provide a mechanism for automatic intermediate state discovery; and (3) a validated iterative protocol requiring only endpoint state simulation data as input. The method is implemented in PLUMED via the PYTORCH_MODEL collective variable, making it directly compatible with GROMACS and other MD engines that support PLUMED. Future work includes extension to multi-dimensional CV outputs, integration with OPES for more efficient production free energy calculations, and application to larger biomolecular systems where the intermediate state discovery capability would be most impactful. The code and models are available in the PLUMED-NEST repository, and the complete workflow is implemented as a Python package for ease of use.
