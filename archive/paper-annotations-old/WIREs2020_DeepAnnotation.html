The challenge of predicting distal active site mutations in computational enzyme design

Sílvia Osuna · WIREs Computational Molecular Science, 2020
DOI: 10.1002/wcms.1502
enzyme engineering distal mutations molecular dynamics allostery computational design
ABS Abstract
ABS

Computational enzyme redesign can be understood as a population shift problem, where the goal is to alter the relative stabilities of different conformational states of the enzyme ensemble and favor those critical for novel function. The challenge of predicting distal mutations—those located far from the active site but critical for function—remains a major bottleneck in the field. Recent computational strategies focus on reconstructing the conformational landscape of enzymes, applying correlation-based tools to identify mutation hotspots. Critically, most important opportunities lie at distal positions, where allosteric effects mediate functional improvements. This review surveys the computational methods that enable rational prediction of such distal mutations for enzyme improvement.

INT Introduction
P1

Enzyme engineering has become a promising strategy for addressing biotechnological challenges, with applications ranging from biofuels to pharmaceuticals. However, creating enzymes with new functions remains difficult, primarily because computational predictions rely heavily on structural information from single crystal structures. These static representations fail to capture the dynamic nature of enzyme function. The vast majority of computational enzyme design efforts have focused on optimizing the immediate chemical environment of the active site, yet experimental results show that residues far from the active site often determine catalytic efficiency. This disconnect between computational expectations and experimental outcomes motivates a fundamental shift in how we approach enzyme design.

P2

The solution lies in adopting an ensemble view of enzyme function. Rather than treating enzymes as static machines, modern computational methods must account for the fact that proteins exist as dynamic ensembles of interconverting conformational states. These different conformations serve different functional roles: some favor substrate binding, others facilitate catalysis, and still others promote product release. The relative populations and exchange rates between these states directly determine catalytic properties. Molecular dynamics simulations provide the primary tool for characterizing this conformational ensemble, offering atomistic insights into which states are thermodynamically accessible and kinetically relevant at physiological conditions.

MTH Methods & Theory
P3

Classical molecular dynamics (MD) simulations constitute the foundation for ensemble characterization. In these simulations, atoms are propagated forward in time using classical mechanics, typically for timescales reaching microseconds to tens of microseconds depending on system size and available computational resources. A key limitation is that the timescales of conformational transitions important for catalysis—often milliseconds or slower—exceed typical MD simulation lengths. Consequently, standard MD alone often fails to sample the rare, functionally relevant conformational states. Despite this limitation, conventional MD provides crucial information about local conformational dynamics and serves as the starting point for more sophisticated approaches. The choice of force field (e.g., AMBER, CHARMM) significantly influences the sampled ensemble, making careful validation essential.

P4

Enhanced sampling methods overcome the timescale limitations of conventional MD. Metadynamics is particularly powerful: it works by adding a bias potential along predefined collective variables (CVs) that define the low-dimensional projection of the conformational space of interest. By depositing Gaussian hills at visited CV values, the method systematically fills the free energy minima, encouraging exploration of high-energy barriers that would otherwise remain inaccessible. The accumulated bias potential converges to minus the free energy surface, providing quantitative thermodynamic information. Other enhanced sampling approaches include accelerated MD, umbrella sampling, and replica exchange methods, each with distinct advantages depending on the system and available resources. These methods enable observation of rare events on tractable computational timescales.

P5

Beyond direct simulation, network-based approaches offer complementary insights into allosteric mechanisms. Shortest Path Map (SPM) analysis constructs a network where residues are nodes and inter-residue contacts are edges, then identifies the shortest paths of contact networks connecting the active site to distal regions. Residues frequently appearing in these shortest paths show high predictive value for identifying mutation hotspots. Dynamic network analysis goes further by incorporating residue fluctuations and correlation information from MD simulations, revealing which portions of the protein show correlated motions indicative of allosteric pathways. These methods predict distal mutations with remarkable accuracy, often identifying specific residues whose mutation enhances function despite their distance from the active site.

APP Applications & Case Studies
P6

Ancestral sequence reconstruction provides a powerful validation strategy for predicted mutations. Phylogenetic analysis identifies likely ancestral sequences at key branch points, and comparison between ancestral and modern enzymes reveals which mutations have accumulated during evolution. Remarkably, evolutionarily preserved residue positions often coincide with computationally predicted distal mutation hotspots. This convergence between computational prediction and evolutionary record suggests that nature has independently discovered many of the same optimization strategies that algorithms now propose. Reversing ancestral mutations or introducing predicted distal mutations into modern enzymes provides experimental validation of mechanistic models. This approach elegantly bridges millions of years of evolution with modern computational enzyme design, providing strong evidence that distal mutations can be rationally predicted and engineered.

P7

Tryptophan synthase represents a canonical example of successful distal mutation prediction. TrpB, the β-subunit, exhibits conformational changes essential for catalysis: an open (O) state that allows substrate binding, a closed (C) state that excludes solvent, and intermediate partially closed (PC) conformations. Experimental and computational work reveals that distal mutations located in the communication (COMM) domain directly affect the relative populations of these states. A small set of COMM domain mutations can shift the conformational equilibrium, substantially improving catalysis in isolated TrpB or enabling function without the α-subunit partner. Free energy calculations and enhanced sampling simulations predicted many of these distal mutations before experimental testing. This success demonstrates that distal mutations can now be rationally predicted and engineering them into enzymes is increasingly practical.
