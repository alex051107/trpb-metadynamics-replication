Sequence-based generative AI design of versatile tryptophan synthases

Lambert T, Tavakoli A, Dharuman G, Yang J, Bhethanabotla V, Kaur S, Hill M, Ramanathan A, Anandkumar A, Arnold FH

ABS Abstract
ABS

Enzymes are powerful and sustainable catalysts, but their widespread application is limited by the difficulty of identifying functional starting points for optimization, creating a major bottleneck in early-stage biocatalyst discovery. Designing libraries of such starting enzymes remains particularly challenging. Here, we use the GenSLM protein language model to generate novel β-subunit of tryptophan synthase (TrpB) enzymes that express in Escherichia coli and are both stable and catalytically active. Many generated TrpBs also display significant substrate promiscuity, outperforming their natural counterparts on non-native substrates. Some even surpass laboratory-evolved TrpBs. Comparison of the most-active and most-promiscuous generated TrpB to its closest natural homolog confirms that the enhanced versatility is absent from the natural enzyme, highlighting the creative potential of generative models. These results establish generative models as powerful tools for biocatalyst discovery and engineering.

§1 Introduction — P1–P3
P1

Enzymes offer attractive alternatives to traditional chemical methods, but industrial application requires tailored optimization. Directed evolution (DE) has emerged as a robust strategy, using iterative rounds of mutagenesis and screening to progressively improve an enzyme toward a desired function. Despite its broad applicability, DE remains fundamentally limited by the need for a starting enzyme with measurable activity toward the target function. Identifying an enzyme with such initial activity is still largely empirical, with no universal strategy currently available. One common approach is to exploit catalytic or substrate promiscuity: hypothesize enzyme families that could accommodate the desired transformation, then construct libraries either by sampling natural sequence diversity or by creating mutant libraries. Both approaches present challenges: natural enzymes may express poorly or have narrow specificity, while mutagenesis covers only a restricted sequence space and frequently yields a high proportion of inactive variants. The process is labor-intensive and time-consuming, with outcomes largely dictated by chance.

P2

To address the challenge of identifying novel enzymes with desired functions, we propose to use protein language models (PLMs) to generate libraries of enzymes that can be screened for target activities. PLM-generated proteins offer significant advantages: they can explore regions of sequence space far from known proteins, while also allowing conditioning or filtering to incorporate desirable features. Recent experimental applications have validated this approach: PLMs can generate functional proteins with real-world relevance. Building on this promise, we used the GenSLM model, originally developed for genome-scale applications. Unlike most protein language models, which are trained on amino acid sequences, GenSLM learns interactions within DNA sequences at the codon level. However, experimental validation of DNA-sequence-based models has so far been limited and remains an open area for exploration.

P3

To assess the model's generative potential, we selected a mechanistically challenging enzyme as a test case. We focused on the β-subunit of tryptophan synthase (TrpB), a subunit of the heterotetrameric tryptophan synthase complex (TrpS). TrpS consists of two TrpA and two TrpB subunits that together catalyze a multistep biosynthetic transformation. TrpB is a PLP-dependent enzyme that condenses indole with L-serine to form L-tryptophan. Efficient catalysis depends on large-scale conformational dynamics between and within subunits, which regulate transitions between low-activity (open) and high-activity (closed) states. A major breakthrough was the creation of PfTrpB-0B2, a stand-alone catalyst whose activity is no longer dependent on its natural partner TrpA. TrpB is also an attractive industrial biocatalyst for synthesizing noncanonical amino acids. Engineered TrpBs are now used at industrial scale, including by Merck in the synthesis of enlicitide decanoate, a Phase 3 clinical candidate.

§2 Methods — GenSLM Pipeline (P4–P5)
P4

The generative AI framework is built on the Genome-Scale Language Model (GenSLM), a large-scale transformer architecture designed to capture biological sequence patterns at the codon level. GenSLM was trained at multiple parameter scales (25M, 250M, 2.5B, and 25B), using a dataset of approximately 110 million prokaryotic gene sequences from the BV-BRC. To generate TrpB sequences, we employed the 25M parameter GenSLM and fine-tuned it on a curated dataset of 30,000 unique trpB DNA sequences (corresponding to 22,800 unique amino acid sequences after translation). Fine-tuning followed the same procedure developed for SARS-CoV-2, using a contrastive learning objective similar to the masked language modeling strategy implemented in SpanBERT. Generated sequences were subjected to a minimal set of filters designed to remove unpromising sequences: length consistency, pLDDT > 0.8 via ESMFold, maximum sequence identity (MaxID) <90% to natural TrpBs, conservation of the catalytic lysine, and diversity via BLAST cluster separation.

P5

Embeddings from the GenSLM model were used to compare the distribution of generated sequences with that of natural TrpB sequences. The generated sequences broadly spanned the natural sequence space used for fine-tuning, as shown by t-SNE projection. Positional variability was highly similar between natural and generated sequences, and key conserved residues in natural TrpBs were retained in the generated sequences. Catalytically active GenSLM-TrpBs were found to be evenly distributed across the natural sequence space, indicating that the model sampled multiple distinct functional solutions rather than converging on a single sequence cluster. From the filtered set, 105 representative sequences were selected and synthesized by Elegen Corp as E. coli codon-optimized DNA sequences.

§3 Results (P6–P8)
P6

Our first objective was to evaluate whether the GenSLM-TrpBs exhibited activity for tryptophan synthesis. We expressed the 105 selected GenSLM-TrpBs in E. coli and compared their performance to several well-characterized controls: natural TrpBs (EcTrpB, AtTrpB, PfTrpB, TmTrpB, SaTrpB) and the lab-evolved PfTrpB-0B2. Assays were performed at room temperature and 75°C. 11 GenSLM-TrpBs showed activity clearly above background at room temperature; 7 retained substantial activity at 75°C. Remarkably, several GenSLM-TrpBs exhibited activity comparable to or even exceeding that of PfTrpB-0B2, a variant specifically evolved for stand-alone function at 75°C. Among these, GenSLM-230 stands out: its total activity surpasses that of PfTrpB-0B2 at both room temperature and 75°C. Average purification yield was 84 mg/L of culture, with four enzymes exceeding 100 mg/L and two approaching 200 mg/L.

P7

We hypothesized that TrpBs generated via a PLM might exhibit broader substrate promiscuity compared with their natural counterparts. Natural enzymes like TrpB are typically highly specific, having undergone evolutionary pressure to suppress promiscuous activities. To investigate this, all 105 GenSLM-TrpBs were screened for activity on a panel of seven non-canonical substrates: 4-NO₂-, 5-NO₂-, 6-CN-, 7-CN-indole, naphthol, L-threonine (as alternative nucleophile), and 5-fluoroindole. These were compared against natural TrpBs and lab-evolved variants specifically engineered for each substrate. For every substrate tested, at least one GenSLM-TrpB exhibited measurable activity. GenSLM-230 was particularly notable: it exhibits measurable activity across all seven tested substrates with yields ranging from 5% to 99%. This degree of substrate promiscuity is unprecedented among natural TrpBs. At 37°C, 230 matched or exceeded the performance of laboratory-evolved enzymes in reactions with 4-NO₂-indole, 5-NO₂-indole, L-threonine, and 7-CN-indole.

P8

To better understand the origin of the properties observed in GenSLM-TrpBs, we asked whether these features merely reflect the natural sequence diversity captured by the model, or whether the model introduces novel attributes. We focused on GenSLM-230 and its closest natural homolog, TrpB from Neobacillus drentensis (NdTrpB), which shares 80.5% sequence identity (322/400 residues). Structural modeling using AlphaFold3 predicted identical folds with a backbone RMSD of 0.36 Å. The active site residues are highly conserved with only one conservative substitution (V→I236). NdTrpB was expressed at higher yield (75 mg/L) than 230 (5 mg/L), but comparable thermal stability (Tm = 76.5°C vs 77.5°C). Catalytically, however, they differed substantially: at 75°C, NdTrpB activity dropped to 19% while 230 retained 94%. More strikingly, 230 exhibited significantly broader reactivity: NdTrpB showed detectable activity only with 7-CN-indole and 5-F-indole, while 230 accepted all tested substrates. This confirms that the enhanced versatility of 230 cannot be explained as simply reproducing a natural enzyme with similar properties.

§4 Discussion (P9)
P9

Protein engineering is undergoing a major transformation driven by advances in AI. While de novo protein design enables the creation of proteins able to catalyze non-natural reactions, this approach remains limited to relatively simple reactions and is currently not applicable to highly complex systems like TrpB, whose mechanism involves two substrates, a cofactor, dynamic conformational changes, and a multistep catalytic cycle. Nevertheless, our results show that GenSLM-TrpBs were expressible, catalytically competent, stable, and broadly promiscuous. The reasons underlying the high activity and increased promiscuity are subtle and difficult to pinpoint. Multiple components of our model could influence the distribution and properties of the generated proteins, including the natural trpB database, the generative model and its pretraining, the fine-tuning strategy, and the filtering applied. We argue that the minimal filtering used here is unlikely to be responsible for the observed high activity: filters were intentionally permissive. Instead, the enhanced promiscuity likely arises from the generation process itself. In an optimized pipeline, the entire cycle of design, gene synthesis, and experimental testing can be completed in as little as one month, providing an exceptionally rapid route to functional enzymes.
